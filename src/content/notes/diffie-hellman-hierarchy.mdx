Three assumptions form a hierarchy that appears throughout modern cryptography, particularly in public key encryption, attribute-based encryption, and beyond.

## Setting up the Problem

Let $G$ be a cyclic group of prime order $p$ with generator $g$.

Choose a secret exponent:

$$
a \in \mathbb{Z}_p
$$

Then compute

$$
g^a.
$$

Everyone can see $g$ and $g^a$.

<Callout type="question" title="Problem">
What can they learn about $a$?
</Callout>

Knowing $a$, the secret, let's the attacker crack protected information.

---

## Discrete Logarithm (DL)

<Definition title="Discrete Logarithm Problem">
Given

$$
g,\ g^a
$$

find

$$
a.
$$
</Definition>

The name comes from ordinary logarithms. We have

$$
g^a = h
$$

and we want

$$
a = \log_g(h).
$$

<Callout type="example" title="Example">
Suppose someone gives:

$$
g,\ g^{1234567}
$$

You want to recover the secret, which is $1234567$. In a sufficiently large group, this is believed to be infeasible.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note" title="Key Remark">
Discrete Log asks for the **entire** secret exponent. This is a lot of information â€” if you can solve DL, you essentially know everything.
</Callout>

---

## Computational Diffie-Hellman (CDH)

Now introduce a second secret exponent. Choose:

$$
a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_p.
$$

Public values:

$$
g,\ g^a,\ g^b.
$$

<Callout type="question" title="Problem">
Solve

$$
g^{ab}.
$$
</Callout>

Alice knows $a$, and Bob knows $b$. They can each compute $g^{ab}$. But an outsider can only see

$$
g,\ g^a,\ g^b.
$$

Can that outsider compute the shared secret? CDH says no efficient algorithm can.

<Callout type="example" title="Example">
Given

$$
g,\ g^7,\ g^{13}
$$

compute

$$
g^{91}.
$$

You don't need $7$ or $13$, just $g^{91}$.
</Callout>

If I solve DL, I know $a$ from $g^a$ and $b$ from $g^b$, so of course I can compute CDH. So:

$$
\text{DL} \implies \text{CDH}.
$$

---

## Decisional Diffie-Hellman (DDH)

Instead of computing $g^{ab}$, we can make the task even easier. You don't need to know the value â€” we only ask:

<Callout type="question" title="Problem">
Can you **recognize** $g^{ab}$?
</Callout>

Given $(g, g^a, g^b, T)$, determine whether

$$
T = g^{ab}
$$

or

$$
T = g^c
$$

for some random $c$.

<Callout type="example" title="Example">
You receive

$$
(g,\ g^7,\ g^{13},\ T).
$$

- Output **yes** if $T = g^{91}$
- Output **no** if $T$ is random

You don't have to compute $g^{91}$.
</Callout>

Of course, if you can compute $g^{ab}$ (the CDH assumption), then you can distinguish it against other values. Thus,

$$
\text{CDH} \implies \text{DDH}.
$$

<Theorem title="The Key Hierarchy">
$$
\text{DL} \implies \text{CDH} \implies \text{DDH}.
$$
</Theorem>

## El-Gamal Encryption: A Real Example of DDH

A classic example is the El-Gamal encryption scheme. Its semantic security (IND-CPA security) is based on the DDH assumption.

### Step 1: Public Key Generation

Alice chooses a secret key

$$
x
$$

and computes her public key

$$
h = g^x.
$$

Public information: $(g,\ h = g^x)$. Secret: $x$.

### Step 2: Encrypting a Message

Suppose Bob wants to send Alice a message $M$. He builds an obfuscation $K$: he picks a fresh random number $r$, then computes

$$
c_1 = g^r
$$

and

$$
K = h^r = (g^x)^r = g^{xr}.
$$

He uses $K$ to hide the message. The ciphertext is:

$$
(c_1,\ M \cdot K).
$$

### Step 3: Decryption

Alice receives $(c_1, c_2)$. She computes

$$
(c_1)^x = (g^r)^x = g^{xr} = K.
$$

Since she now knows $K$, she removes it from the ciphertext and recovers $M$.

---

## Where DDH Comes In

Imagine you're an attacker. You see:

$$
(g,\ g^x,\ g^r,\ g^{xr}).
$$

The first two, $g$ and $g^x$, are public. The values $g^r$ and $g^{xr}$ are what Bob sent for the ciphertext $(c_1, c_2)$, which you intercepted. This is exactly the Diffie-Hellman tuple!

The attacker doesn't know whether $g^{xr}$ is the genuine Diffie-Hellman secret or just some random group element. The DDH assumption says you can't distinguish those two cases.

<Callout type="question" title="Question">
How does that protect the message?
</Callout>

Suppose we replace the real shared secret $g^{xr}$ with a completely random group element $g^z$. The ciphertext becomes

$$
(g^r,\ M \cdot g^z).
$$

Since $g^z$ is random, it acts like a **one-time pad** in the group. The second part of the ciphertext reveals nothing about $M$.

If the attacker could tell the difference between a ciphertext using $g^{xr}$ and some random garbage $g^z$, they would have to be able to distinguish a real Diffie-Hellman tuple from a random one â€” which breaks DDH. Since they can't tell real from fake encryption, they can't learn anything useful about the data.

---

## Concrete Security Game

<Callout type="example" title="The Guessing Game">
Suppose Eve intercepted a ciphertext and knows it encrypts one of two messages:

- Transfer 10$ (real)
- Transfer literally any other amount of dollars

Her goal is to guess which one. If El-Gamal is secure, her probability of guessing correctly is essentially as good as flipping a coin â€” since distinguishing the two ciphertexts would require distinguishing a real DH computation from a random one.
</Callout>
