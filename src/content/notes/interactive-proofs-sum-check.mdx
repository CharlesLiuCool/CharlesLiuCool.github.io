## The Big Picture

These are my notes from MIT Open-Course Ware class **"Advanced Cryptography"** taught by Yael Tauman Kalai.

1. **Interactive Proofs (IP).** The verifier is efficient (polynomial time), while the prover may be all-powerful. By tradition the prover is called *Merlin* (a wizard, all-powerful) and the verifier is *Arthur* (the king).
2. **Doubly-Efficient IP (GKR).** A real-world prover is never all-powerful, so we ask the prover to also run efficiently.
3. **Applications.** Probabilistically-Checkable Proofs (PCP), Multiprover Interactive Proofs (MIP), and the celebrated theorem $\text{IP} = \text{PSPACE}$.
4. **Cryptography for succinct arguments.** We trade absolute soundness ("if the theorem is false there is *no* proof") for computational soundness ("to prove a false theorem you must break a cryptographic assumption"). This buys us succinctness.
5. **Fiat–Shamir.** A paradigm for removing interaction, turning a doubly-efficient interactive proof into a non-interactive one and enabling succinct batch arguments.
6. **Non-interactive GKR / batch arguments**, and finally the **SNARG**.

<Callout type="note" title="Roadmap">
$$
\text{IP} \to \text{GKR} \to \text{(PCP, MIP, IP=PSPACE)} \to \text{Succinct Arguments} 
$$
$$
\to \text{Fiat–Shamir} \to \text{SNARG}
$$
</Callout>

## Complexity Classes

<Definition title="NP">
The set of languages such that membership can be proved to a polynomial-time verifier.
</Definition>

This is the classical proof model. Interactive proofs generalize it along two axes.

<Definition title="Interactive Proofs (IP)">
The proof model that additionally allows:

- **interaction** between prover and verifier, and
- **randomization** by the verifier.
</Definition>

Because a classical NP proof is just a degenerate interaction, we immediately have

$$
\text{NP} \subseteq \text{IP}.
$$

Randomization means there is some (very small) chance the verifier accepts a false statement. Both ingredients matter:

- **No randomization.** If the verifier were deterministic, the prover could generate the entire transcript on its own and hand it over — so we are back to plain NP.
- **Randomization but no interaction.** A non-interactive proof with a randomized verifier has its own name: **Merlin–Arthur (MA)**.

<Callout type="example" title="An MA problem — verifying a matrix product">
Let $A, B, C$ be $n \times n$ matrices over a finite field $\mathbb{F}$. We want to verify whether $C = A \cdot B$.

Deterministically, the best known matrix-multiplication algorithms cost about $O(n^{2.37})$. A randomized check does better.

**Randomized algorithm (Freivalds).** Assume without loss of generality $|\mathbb{F}| \gg n$ (we can always enlarge the field via extensions). Pick $r \in \mathbb{F}$ and set

$$
x = (1, r, r^2, \dots, r^{n-1}).
$$

Then check

$$
C x^\top \stackrel{?}{=} A B x^\top.
$$

**Analysis.** Each side is a matrix–vector product, costing $O(n^2)$ — faster than full multiplication. Each entry of $Cx^\top$ and $ABx^\top$ is a degree-$(n-1)$ polynomial in $r$ whose coefficients come from $C$ and $AB$ respectively. We only accept a false claim when two distinct degree-$(n-1)$ polynomials agree at $r$, which happens at most at $n-1$ points. Hence the failure probability is at most

$$
\frac{n-1}{|\mathbb{F}|},
$$

which we drive down by enlarging $\mathbb{F}$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note" title="Remark">
This matrix check is the univariate special case of the **Schwartz–Zippel Lemma**, which generalizes it to the multivariate setting.
</Callout>

<Definition title="Interactive Proof (formal)">
An *interactive proof* for a language $L$ is a protocol between an (all-powerful) prover $P$ and an efficient (polynomial-time) verifier $V$, with completeness $C$ and soundness $S$, such that, writing $\text{tr} = \langle P, V(r)\rangle(x)$ for the transcript on input $x$ and randomness $r$:

- **Completeness:** for all $x \in L$,
$$
\Pr\big[V(\text{tr}, r, x) = 1\big] \ge C.
$$
- **Soundness:** for all $x \notin L$ and all (cheating) provers $P^*$,
$$
\Pr\big[V(\text{tr}, r, x) = 1\big] \le S.
$$
</Definition>

$C$ and $S$ are parameters. As in BPP one might start with $C = \tfrac{2}{3}$ and $S = \tfrac{1}{3}$; these gaps can be amplified by repeating the protocol.

<Callout type="note" title="History">
Later we will see $\text{IP} = \text{PSPACE}$, a celebrated result of Shamir (1990). The model was originally introduced to study zero-knowledge, but this interactive, randomized world turned out to prove far more than a polynomial-time verifier was ever believed capable of.
</Callout>

## The Sum-Check Protocol

This is perhaps *the* most important protocol here: an interactive proof for sum-check bootstraps interactive proofs for all of PSPACE.

<Definition title="Sum-Check">
Given a polynomial

$$
f : \mathbb{F}^m \to \mathbb{F}
$$

of degree $d$ in each variable, to which we have oracle access, and a fixed subset $H \subseteq \mathbb{F}$, the prover must convince the verifier that

$$
\sum_{h_1, \dots, h_m \in H} f(h_1, \dots, h_m) = \beta
$$

for a claimed value $\beta$.
</Definition>

The protocol proceeds in $m$ rounds, eliminating one variable per round. In round $i$ the prover sends a univariate polynomial $g_i$ (the sum over the remaining variables with $x_i$ left free); the verifier checks it is consistent with the previous claim, then sends a fresh random challenge $t_i \leftarrow \mathbb{F}$ to fix $x_i$. We start with an $m$-variate claim and end with a single univariate check that the verifier can evaluate against the oracle.

**Completeness.** An honest prover holding the correct polynomial always passes, so completeness is $1$.

**Soundness.** Consider a cheating prover. Its first message $g_1$ must already be wrong (a correct $g_1$ would require knowing the true polynomial), so we begin in a "false" state. To convince the verifier at the end, at some round the prover must switch onto a genuinely correct polynomial that happens to line up with the induced randomness: at round $i$ the message $g_i$ is bad, but $g_{i+1}$ is good and matches the challenge $t_i \leftarrow \mathbb{F}$.

The event that a single round's cheat survives is

$$
\sum_{h \in H} g_{i+1}(h) = g_i(t_i),
$$

i.e. two distinct degree-$d$ polynomials agree at the random point $t_i$, which happens for at most $d$ of the $|\mathbb{F}|$ choices. Union bounding over the $m$ rounds,

$$
\Pr[P^* \text{ cheats successfully}] \le \frac{md}{|\mathbb{F}|},
$$

which is amplified away by enlarging the field.

<Callout type="note" title="Why polynomials?">
Polynomials are special because low-degree extension lets us embed many problems into them, and because of their error-correcting behavior: if a cheating prover is wrong at some point $t_i$, that error is *amplified* — a wrong polynomial disagrees almost everywhere.
</Callout>

<Definition title="Public-Coin Protocol">
A protocol in which every verifier message is simply uniformly random bits. Sum-check is public-coin.
</Definition>

## Application: $\#\text{SAT} \in \text{IP}$

We now show that "sharp-SAT" has an interactive proof.

Let $\phi$ be a Boolean formula over $x_1, \dots, x_n$ built from AND, OR, NOT. View it as a binary tree whose leaves are the variables (a variable may appear at several leaves). Then

$$
(\phi, k) \in \#\text{SAT} \iff \big|\{\, x \in \{0,1\}^n : \phi(x) = 1 \,\}\big| = k,
$$

i.e. $\phi$ has exactly $k$ satisfying assignments. Let $S$ denote the *size* of $\phi$, its number of leaves.

<Callout type="note" title="Formula vs. circuit">
A formula is a binary *tree*, whereas a circuit is an arbitrary directed acyclic graph. A formula therefore repeats a lot of computation that a circuit could share. Counting satisfying assignments is a notoriously hard problem.
</Callout>

**Key idea — arithmetization.** Lift $\phi$ into a polynomial over a finite field $\mathbb{F}$ by replacing each gate:

$$
\text{AND}(x, y) = x \cdot y,
$$
$$
\text{OR}(x, y) = x + y - x \cdot y,
$$
$$
\text{NOT}(x) = 1 - x.
$$

Write $\tilde{\phi}$ for the arithmetized polynomial. It then suffices to prove

$$
\sum_{b_1, \dots, b_n \in \{0,1\}} \tilde{\phi}(b_1, \dots, b_n) = k,
$$

which is exactly a sum-check instance over $H = \{0,1\}$.

The reason this works: $\tilde{\phi}\big|_{\{0,1\}^n} \equiv \phi$. On Boolean inputs the arithmetized gates behave cleanly and reproduce AND, OR, NOT exactly, so $\tilde\phi$ agrees with $\phi$ on the hypercube. Off the hypercube $\tilde\phi$ may behave wildly, but we never sum there.

<Theorem title="Degree bound of the arithmetization">
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n} \deg_i(\tilde{\phi}) \le S,
$$

where $\deg_i$ is the degree of variable $x_i$ in $\tilde\phi$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
By induction on the structure of $\phi$.

*Base case.* A single-leaf formula is arithmetized to a degree-$1$ polynomial, and $S = 1$.

*Inductive step.* Suppose $\phi$ is a gate applied to subformulas $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$, of sizes $S_1$ and $S_2$ with $S = S_1 + S_2$. In either the AND case, $\tilde\phi = \tilde\phi_1 \cdot \tilde\phi_2$, or the OR case, $\tilde\phi = \tilde\phi_1 + \tilde\phi_2 - \tilde\phi_1 \cdot \tilde\phi_2$, the degree in each variable is bounded by the sum of the degrees in the two subformulas. Hence

$$
\sum_i \deg_i(\tilde\phi) \le \sum_i \deg_i(\tilde\phi_1) + \sum_i \deg_i(\tilde\phi_2) \le S_1 + S_2 = S.
$$
</Proof>

Running sum-check on this instance gives a verifier that works in time polynomial in $n$ and $\log|\mathbb{F}|$, with soundness error

$$
\frac{n \cdot S}{|\mathbb{F}|}.
$$

## Low-Degree Extension

Arithmetization is one instance of a general and fundamental tool.

<Theorem title="Low-Degree Extension">
Let $f : H^m \to \{0,1\}$ be any function (think of it as an arbitrary array of $|H|^m$ bits laid out on a hypercube), and let $\mathbb{F}$ be any finite field with $H \subseteq \mathbb{F}$. Then there exists a **unique**

$$
\tilde{f} : \mathbb{F}^m \to \mathbb{F}
$$

such that $\tilde{f}\big|_{H^m} = f$ and $\tilde{f}$ has degree $\le |H| - 1$ in each variable. Explicitly,

$$
\tilde{f}(z) = \sum_{h_1, \dots, h_m \in H} f(h_1, \dots, h_m)\, \chi_{h_1, \dots, h_m}(z),
\qquad
\chi_{h_1, \dots, h_m}(x_1, \dots, x_m) = \prod_{i=1}^{m} \chi_{h_i}(x_i).
$$

Here each factor $\chi_{h_i}$ is the univariate **Lagrange basis polynomial** over $H$ that indicates the point $h_i$: for all $h' \in H$,

$$
\chi_{h_i}(h') = \begin{cases} 1 & h' = h_i \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases}
\qquad
\chi_{h_i}(x_i) = \prod_{\substack{h' \in H \\ h' \ne h_i}} \frac{h' - x_i}{h' - h_i}.
$$

The numerator vanishes whenever $x_i$ hits any point of $H$ other than $h_i$, and the denominator normalizes $\chi_{h_i}(h_i) = 1$; it has degree $|H| - 1$.
</Theorem>

Specializing to $m = 1$ recovers the familiar interpolation theorem.

<Theorem title="Lagrange Interpolation">
For $f : H \to \{0,1\}$ there is a unique

$$
\tilde{f} : \mathbb{F} \to \mathbb{F}
$$

of degree $|H| - 1$, given by

$$
\tilde{f}(x) = \sum_{h \in H} f(h) \cdot \chi_h(x),
$$

where the Lagrange basis polynomial satisfies, for all $h' \in H$,

$$
\chi_h(h') = \begin{cases} 1 & h' = h \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases}
\qquad
\chi_h(x) = \prod_{\substack{h' \in H \\ h' \ne h}} \frac{h' - x}{h' - h}.
$$
</Theorem>

<Proof>
*Agreement on the hypercube.* For any $(h_1^*, \dots, h_m^*) \in H^m$, the product $\chi_{h_1, \dots, h_m}$ is $1$ exactly when $(h_1, \dots, h_m) = (h_1^*, \dots, h_m^*)$ and $0$ otherwise, so

$$
\tilde{f}(h_1^*, \dots, h_m^*) = \sum_{h_1, \dots, h_m} f(h_1, \dots, h_m)\, \chi_{h_1, \dots, h_m}(h_1^*, \dots, h_m^*) = f(h_1^*, \dots, h_m^*).
$$

*Degree.* Since $\chi_{h_1, \dots, h_m}$ factors as $\prod_i \chi_{h_i}(x_i)$ and each $\chi_{h_i}$ is univariate of degree $|H| - 1$,

$$
\deg_i(\tilde{f}) = \deg\big(\chi_{h_i}(x_i)\big) = |H| - 1.
$$
</Proof>
